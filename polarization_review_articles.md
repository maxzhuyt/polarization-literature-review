## Summary of Key Themes

1. **Annual Review of Political Science** is the single most important venue for polarization reviews (Fiorina & Abrams 2008; Layman, Carsey & Horowitz 2006; McCarty 2019; Iyengar et al. 2019; Prior 2013).

2. **Affective polarization** has dominated since ~2012 (Iyengar, Sood & Lelkes 2012), with the Iyengar et al. (2019) ARPS review and Finkel et al. (2020) *Science* piece as key syntheses.

3. **Sorting vs. polarization** is a central conceptual debate (Fiorina vs. Abramowitz; Levendusky 2009; Mason 2015, 2018).

4. **Media/social media effects** remain contested: Boxell, Gentzkow & Shapiro (2017) and Bruns (2019) provide skeptical counterpoints; Bail et al. (2018) shows backfire effects; Lorenz-Spreen et al. (2023) provides the most comprehensive recent systematic review.

5. **Comparative work** has expanded rapidly (McCoy et al. 2018; Somer & McCoy 2019; Gidron et al. 2020; Boxell et al. 2022).

6. **Psychology of polarization** draws on motivated reasoning (Lodge & Taber 2013; Kahan 2013), group polarization (Isenberg 1986; Sunstein 2002), and identity theory (Green et al. 2002; Van Bavel & Pereira 2018).

7. **Measurement foundations**: DiMaggio et al. (1996) established the empirical framework for tracking polarization trends; Bramson et al. (2016) formalized nine distinct polarization measures; Gentzkow, Shapiro & Taddy (2019) developed text-based approaches.

8. **NLP and computational text analysis** (Section X): A growing field applying machine learning, topic models, and embeddings to congressional speech, social media, and news data to measure partisanship and polarization at scale. Németh (2023) reviews 154 studies, finding U.S. bias (59%), Twitter dominance (43%), and machine learning approaches (33%) as key patterns.
